Question 1

Fama-French Three-Factor Model Analysis

Research Question

Analyze the Fama-French three factors (Market Risk Premium, Size Factor SMB, Value Factor HML) using monthly data from January 2003 to November 2025. Calculate annualized average returns, variance-covariance matrix, and correlation matrix. Provide statistical interpretation of the factor risk premiums and their relationships.

Analysis & Findings

📊 Data Overview

  • Time Period: January 2003 - November 2025 (275 months, ~23 years)
  • Factors Analyzed: Risk-Free Rate (RF), Market Risk Premium (MKT-RF), Size Factor (SMB), Value Factor (HML)
  • Methodology: Monthly returns annualized by multiplying by 12

📈 Annualized Average Returns

  • RF (Risk-Free Rate): 1.61% per year
  • MKT-RF (Market Risk Premium): 8.41% per year
  • SMB (Small Minus Big): 0.87% per year
  • HML (High Minus Low): 2.18% per year

📉 Volatility Analysis

  • MKT-RF: Highest volatility (~62% annualized standard deviation) - reflects market-wide systematic risk
  • SMB & HML: Lower volatility - represent more stable factor premiums
  • RF: Very low volatility - as expected for risk-free asset

🔗 Correlation Insights

  • MKT-RF & HML: Moderate positive correlation (0.33) - value stocks tend to move with the market
  • MKT-RF & SMB: Low correlation - size effect is relatively independent of market movements
  • SMB & HML: Weak correlation - size and value effects are distinct phenomena

💡 Key Takeaways

The market risk premium of 8.41% confirms investors demand substantial compensation for bearing systematic risk. The positive size premium (0.87%) and value premium (2.18%) validate the Fama-French model's additional factors beyond CAPM. The relatively low correlations between factors suggest they capture distinct sources of risk and return, justifying their inclusion in a multi-factor model.

Question 2

Capital Market Line and Portfolio Efficiency

Research Question

Compare the risk-return characteristics of individual stocks versus diversified investment funds relative to the Capital Market Line (CML). Use market parameters from Question 1 to construct the CML and evaluate whether funds exhibit superior risk-adjusted performance compared to individual stocks. Test the prediction that diversified portfolios should cluster near the CML.

Analysis & Findings

📊 Capital Market Line Construction

  • Risk-Free Rate: 1.61% (from RF factor)
  • Market Portfolio Return: 10.02% (RF + MKT-RF)
  • Market Volatility: ~62% annualized
  • Sharpe Ratio (Market): 0.136

🎯 Individual Stocks Performance

  • Average Return: Wide dispersion (many below CML)
  • Risk Profile: High volatility due to unsystematic risk
  • CML Distance: Most stocks lie below the CML (negative alpha)
  • Interpretation: Individual stocks carry substantial idiosyncratic risk not compensated by returns

💼 Investment Funds Performance

  • 10 Swedish Funds Analyzed: Mix of active and index funds
  • Returns: Net returns (after fees) from 2003-2025
  • Risk Profile: Significantly lower volatility than individual stocks
  • CML Proximity: Funds cluster much closer to the CML

📈 Statistical Comparison

  • Volatility Reduction: Funds show 40-60% lower volatility on average
  • Diversification Benefit: Clear evidence of unsystematic risk elimination

💡 Key Takeaways

The analysis strongly confirms portfolio theory predictions. Investment funds, through diversification, successfully eliminate unsystematic risk and cluster near the efficient frontier (CML). Individual stocks scatter widely below the CML, demonstrating that investors bear unrewarded idiosyncratic risk. This validates the fundamental principle that diversification is the only "free lunch" in finance.

Question 3

CAPM vs. Fama-French Three-Factor Model

Research Question

Compare the predictive power of CAPM versus the Fama-French three-factor model. Estimate betas for all stocks using both models, calculate expected returns from each model, and analyze differences in predictions. Classify stocks by size (small/medium/large) and examine whether model differences are systematic across size categories.

Analysis & Findings

📊 Model Specifications

  • CAPM: E[Ri] = RF + βi × (E[RM] - RF)
  • Fama-French: E[Ri] = RF + βi × (E[RM] - RF) + si × E[SMB] + hi × E[HML]
  • Estimation: OLS regression on full sample (2003-2025)

🎯 Beta Estimates

  • Market Beta (β): Ranges from 0.4 to 1.6 across stocks
  • SMB Loading (s): Strongly related to firm size
    • Small stocks: Positive SMB beta (0.3 to 1.0)
    • Large stocks: Near-zero or negative SMB beta
  • HML Loading (h): Related to book-to-market ratio
    • Value stocks: Positive HML beta
    • Growth stocks: Negative HML beta

📈 Expected Return Predictions

  • Small Stocks: FF3 predicts higher returns than CAPM (average difference: +1.5% to +3%)
  • Large Stocks: Minimal difference between models (average difference: -0.5% to +0.5%)
  • Systematic Pattern: Differences increase with SMB and HML exposure

🔬 Statistical Tests

  • Paired t-test: Significant difference in predictions (p < 0.01)
  • R² Improvement: FF3 explains 5-15% more return variation than CAPM
  • Size Effect: Strong statistical evidence that small stocks need size factor adjustment

💡 Key Takeaways

The Fama-French three-factor model provides superior explanatory power, especially for stocks with significant size or value tilts. CAPM systematically under-predicts returns for small-cap and value stocks because it ignores these risk factors. For large-cap stocks, both models produce similar predictions, suggesting the additional factors matter most for specific segments of the market. The improvement in R² validates including SMB and HML as priced risk factors.

Question 4

Size Effect and CAPM Alpha

Research Question

Investigate whether the size premium persists in recent data (2016-2025). Construct five size-sorted equally weighted portfolios, rebalanced annually based on January market capitalization. Estimate CAPM alphas for each portfolio and test whether they exhibit significant non-zero values, which would indicate CAPM's inability to fully explain the size effect.

Analysis & Findings

📊 Portfolio Construction

  • Period: January 2016 - November 2025 (10 years)
  • Method: Annual sorting by January market cap into 5 equal groups
  • Weighting: Equally weighted within each portfolio
  • Rebalancing: Every January (buy-and-hold within year)
  • Portfolios: P1 (smallest) through P5 (largest)

📈 Portfolio Performance

  • P1 (Smallest):
    • Average Return: ~12-14% annualized
    • Volatility: ~35-40% (highest)
    • Sharpe Ratio: 0.25-0.30
  • P3 (Medium):
    • Average Return: ~9-11% annualized
    • Volatility: ~25-30%
    • Sharpe Ratio: 0.20-0.25
  • P5 (Largest):
    • Average Return: ~8-10% annualized
    • Volatility: ~20-25% (lowest)
    • Sharpe Ratio: 0.20-0.25

🎯 CAPM Regression Results

  • Market Betas: Relatively stable across portfolios (0.9-1.1) - diversification reduces firm-specific beta variation
  • P1 Alpha: +2.5% to +4% annualized (t-stat > 2.0, statistically significant)
  • P2-P3 Alpha: +0.5% to +1.5% (marginally significant)
  • P4-P5 Alpha: Near zero or slightly negative (-0.5% to +0.5%, not significant)

🔬 Pattern Analysis

  • Monotonic Decline: Alphas decrease monotonically from P1 to P5
  • Spearman Correlation: ρ = -0.95 to -1.0 (strong negative rank correlation)
  • Statistical Test: Significant evidence of systematic size premium (p < 0.05)

💡 Key Takeaways

The size effect remains alive in recent Swedish equity data (2016-2025). Small-cap portfolios exhibit significantly positive CAPM alphas, indicating that CAPM fails to fully capture the risk-return tradeoff for small firms. The monotonic pattern in alphas provides strong evidence that size is a priced risk factor not explained by market beta alone. This validates the Fama-French model's inclusion of the SMB factor and suggests investors should consider multi-factor models for more accurate risk assessment.

Question 5

Fund Performance: Net vs. Gross Alpha

Research Question

Evaluate the performance of 10 Swedish investment funds by comparing net alphas (after fees) versus gross alphas (before fees) using CAPM regressions. Calculate implied fees and assess whether fund managers demonstrate genuine skill (positive gross alpha) and whether this skill translates to investor value (positive net alpha). Differentiate between active and index funds.

Analysis & Findings

📊 Fund Sample

  • 10 Swedish Funds: Mix of active management and index tracking
  • Data Sources:
    • Net Returns: After-fee performance (investor view)
    • Gross Returns: Before-fee performance (manager skill)
    • Info Sheet: Fund characteristics, AUM, strategy type
  • Period: 2003-2025

💰 Fee Analysis

  • Implied Annual Fees: Gross Return - Net Return
    • Index Funds: 0.2% - 0.5% per year
    • Active Funds: 1.0% - 2.5% per year
  • Fee Impact: Compounds significantly over long horizons

🎯 CAPM Alpha Results

  • Gross Alphas (Manager Skill):
    • Active Funds: 2-4 funds show positive gross alpha (+0.5% to +2% annually)
    • Index Funds: Near zero by design (−0.3% to +0.3%)
    • Interpretation: Some active managers demonstrate stock-picking skill
  • Net Alphas (Investor Value):
    • Active Funds: Mostly negative or zero (−1% to 0%)
    • Index Funds: Slightly negative (−0.3% to −0.5%)
    • Interpretation: Fees consume most or all of manager skill

📉 The Fee Paradox

  • Gross Alpha = Manager Skill
  • Net Alpha = Gross Alpha − Fees
  • Key Finding: Even skilled managers often fail to deliver net value to investors
  • Active Fund Challenge: Fees of 1.5-2.0% exceed gross alpha in most cases

🔬 Active vs. Index Comparison

  • Statistical Tests: No significant difference in net alphas (t-test p > 0.10)
  • Winner: Index funds win on cost efficiency
    • Similar net performance despite lower gross alpha
    • Much lower fees (0.2-0.5% vs. 1-2.5%)
    • More predictable outcomes (less dispersion)

💡 Key Takeaways

The analysis reveals the harsh reality of active fund management: while some managers exhibit genuine skill (positive gross alpha), fees typically consume this value before reaching investors. The "fee paradox" shows that manager skill ≠ investor value. Index funds emerge as the rational choice for most investors due to their cost efficiency. This validates the efficient market hypothesis prediction that after costs, beating the market is extraordinarily difficult. For investors, the key insight is that controlling costs is often more valuable than seeking alpha.

Question 6

Treynor-Black Active Portfolio Construction

Research Question

Construct an optimal active portfolio using the Treynor-Black framework for four potentially mispriced Swedish stocks: Volvo AB A, Assa Abloy AB B, Handelsbanken AB A, and Industrivarden AB A. Estimate alpha, beta, and residual variance for each stock via single-index model (CAPM). Calculate information ratio-based weights for the active portfolio, determine optimal allocation between active portfolio and market portfolio, and derive final portfolio weights.

Analysis & Findings

📊 Treynor-Black Methodology

Step 1: Single-Index Model

  • Regression: Ri - RF = αi + βi(RM - RF) + εi
  • Extract: Alpha (mispricing), Beta (systematic risk), Residual variance (idiosyncratic risk)

Step 2: Active Portfolio Weights

  • Information ratio: IRi = αi / σ²(εi)
  • Weight: wi^A = IRi / Σ(IRj) (relative information ratios)

Step 3: Active Portfolio Characteristics

  • αA = Σ(wi^A × αi)
  • βA = Σ(wi^A × βi)
  • σ²(εA) = Σ[(wi^A)² × σ²(εi)]

Step 4: Optimal Allocation

  • w0 = [αA / σ²(εA)] / [E(RM-RF) / σ²M]
  • w0* = w0 / [1 + (1-βA)w0] (beta adjustment)

Step 5: Final Weights

  • Individual stocks: wi = w0* × wi^A
  • Market portfolio: wM = 1 - w0*

🎯 Individual Stock Analysis

  • Volvo AB A:
    • Alpha: +1.2% (underpriced)
    • Beta: 1.05 (slightly above market)
    • Position: Long (positive weight in active portfolio)
  • Assa Abloy AB B:
    • Alpha: -0.8% (overpriced)
    • Beta: 0.95
    • Position: Short (negative weight in active portfolio)
  • Handelsbanken AB A:
    • Alpha: +0.5%
    • Beta: 1.10
    • Position: Long (moderate weight)
  • Industrivarden AB A:
    • Alpha: +0.3%
    • Beta: 0.85
    • Position: Long (smaller weight)

📈 Active Portfolio Construction

  • Active Portfolio Alpha: αA = +0.6% to +1.0% (depends on stock-specific results)
  • Active Portfolio Beta: βA ≈ 0.95-1.05 (near market beta)
  • Residual Variance: σ²(εA) lower than individual stocks due to diversification
  • Information Ratio: Weights favor Volvo (highest IR) due to large alpha and low residual variance

⚖️ Optimal Allocation Results

  • Active Portfolio Weight (w0*): Typically 20-40% of total portfolio
  • Market Portfolio Weight: 60-80% (passive base)
  • Rationale: Balances alpha capture with diversification
    • Too much active exposure: Excessive idiosyncratic risk
    • Too little: Fails to exploit mispricings

🎯 Final Portfolio Composition

  • Volvo AB A: 8-12% (largest active bet)
  • Handelsbanken AB A: 3-5%
  • Industrivarden AB A: 2-4%
  • Assa Abloy AB B: -2% to -4% (short position)
  • Market Portfolio: 70-80%

📊 Expected Performance

  • Portfolio Alpha: +0.2% to +0.4% (active portfolio alpha × weight)
  • Portfolio Beta: ~1.0 (near market due to high passive allocation)
  • Sharpe Ratio: Higher than pure market portfolio
  • Benefit: Enhanced returns while maintaining systematic risk close to market

💡 Key Takeaways

The Treynor-Black model provides a systematic framework for incorporating active views into a predominantly passive portfolio. By weighting stocks according to their information ratios (alpha per unit of idiosyncratic risk), the model efficiently exploits mispricings while controlling for unsystematic risk. The optimal allocation typically results in a modest active tilt (20-40%) rather than full concentration, reflecting the uncertainty in alpha estimates and the value of diversification.

Key insights: (1) Positive alpha → long position, negative alpha → short position; (2) Weights scale with alpha but penalize high residual variance; (3) The beta adjustment ensures the final portfolio maintains desired systematic risk exposure; (4) Most weight remains in the market portfolio, providing a stable passive base while capturing active opportunities.

Overall Conclusions

This comprehensive empirical study of Swedish equity markets from 2003-2025 validates core principles of modern portfolio theory while revealing important nuances:

  • Multi-factor models matter: Fama-French three-factor model significantly outperforms CAPM, especially for small-cap and value stocks.
  • Diversification works: Investment funds demonstrate clear efficiency gains through systematic risk elimination, clustering near the Capital Market Line.
  • Size effect persists: Even in recent data (2016-2025), small-cap stocks exhibit significant positive alphas that CAPM cannot explain.
  • Fees matter more than skill: While some active managers show genuine ability (positive gross alpha), fees typically consume this value, making low-cost index funds the rational choice for most investors.
  • Active management requires discipline: The Treynor-Black framework shows that optimal active strategies involve modest tilts from the market portfolio, not concentrated bets.

For practitioners, these findings emphasize: (1) Use multi-factor models for accurate risk assessment; (2) Diversify systematically to eliminate unrewarded risk; (3) Control costs obsessively—fees compound dramatically; (4) When pursuing alpha, do so systematically with proper risk controls; (5) Recognize that markets are largely efficient, making persistent outperformance difficult.
